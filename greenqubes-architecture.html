How it gets smarter every week

The company notebook + Monday morning ritual

What we write in Obsidian
The team's notebook

Anything we've figured out that we don't want to forget. The kind of know-how that usually lives in someone's head — until they leave.

  • 📐
    Install how-tos "How to mount LED on curved walls — Aaron, MBS Hall B"
  • 👤
    Client profiles "Uniqlo SG — contact Sarah Chen, prefers IJ180 vinyl, hates last-minute calls"
  • 💰
    Supplier list sales-only "Vinyl supplier A — $4.50/sqft, 2-day lead, COD"
  • 📋
    After-job notes "Capitaland Plaza — goods lift double-booked, ran 2hr over. Confirm slot 48hr ahead next time."
  • 📞
    Internal contacts "BCA hoarding queries → Mr. Lim, ext. 4421"
  • 📄
    Templates "Quote template, PTW template, onboarding checklist"
Some things are sales-only. Supplier prices and quotes have a tag at the top of the file that hides them from installers. The chatbot respects this — installers literally cannot get a price answer, even if they ask.
The Monday morning ritual
5 minutes a week, smarter forever

Every chat with our chatbot quietly gets tagged. Once a week, the system surfaces the ones worth remembering. One click promotes them into the company notebook.

1
MON–FRI
Team chats with the bot. Aaron asks how to install LED on a curved wall. Solves it on-site. Tells the bot what worked.
↓

The bot quietly tags it. Topic: install. Importance: 4/5. New technique solved a problem.
↓

You get a Telegram digest. "3 chats from last week look worth promoting. Skim?" Each one is a one-paragraph summary.
↓
4
YOUR CALL
Promote it — or don't. One tap turns the chat into a permanent notebook entry. Useless ones stay buried in the chat archive.
↓
★
FOREVER
Now in the notebook. Next time anyone asks "how do you install LED on curved walls?" — the bot just knows.
The point: the system doesn't auto-decide what's important. You do, in 5 minutes a week. This is a natural fit for the morning meetings we don't have yet — the digest can become the agenda.
Is this normal?
Is it normal to use nine different services?

Yes — completely normal, and it's how every modern app is built. The thinking is simple: each service we use is the best in the world at one specific job. We pick the best photo locker, the best filing cabinet, the best chatbot brain, etc., and stitch them together.

The alternative — finding "one big platform that does everything" — sounds simpler but is almost always more expensive, slower, and less reliable. Specialists beat generalists.

The trade-off is more accounts to manage. The win is roughly 10× lower cost, faster app, and the ability to swap any one piece in future without rebuilding everything from scratch.
